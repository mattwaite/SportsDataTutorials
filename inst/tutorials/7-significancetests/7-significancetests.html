Significance tests

Now that we‚Äôve worked with data a little, it‚Äôs time to start asking more probing questions of our data. One of the most probing questions we can ask ‚Äì one that so few sports journalists ask ‚Äì is if the difference between this thing and the normal thing is real.

We had a perfect natural experiment going on in sports to show how significance tests work. The NBA in 2021, to salvage a season and get to the playoffs, put their players in a bubble ‚Äì more accurately a hotel complex at Disney World in Orlando ‚Äì and had them play games without fans.

So are the games different from other regular season games that had fans?

To answer this, we need to understand that a significance test is a way to determine if two numbers are significantly different from each other. Generally speaking, we‚Äôre asking if a subset of data ‚Äì a sample ‚Äì is different from the total data pool ‚Äì the population. Typically, this relies on data being in a normal distribution.

If it is, then we know certain things about it. Like the mean ‚Äì the average ‚Äì will be a line right at the peak of cases. And that 66 percent of cases will be in that red area ‚Äì the first standard deviation.

A significance test will determine if a sample taken from that group is different from the total.

Significance testing involves stating a hypothesis. In our case, our hypothesis is that there is a difference between bubble games without people and regular games with people.

In statistics, the null hypothesis is the opposite of your hypothesis. In this case, that there is no difference between fans and no fans.

What we‚Äôre driving toward is a metric called a p-value, which is the probability that you‚Äôd get your sample mean if the null hypothesis is true. So in our case, it‚Äôs the probability we‚Äôd see the numbers we get if there was no difference between fans and no fans. If that probability is below .05, then we consider the difference significant and we reject the null hypothesis.

So let‚Äôs see. We‚Äôll need a log of every game last NBA season. In this data, there‚Äôs a field called COVID, which labels the game as a regular game or a bubble game.
